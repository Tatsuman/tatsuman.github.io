Abstract Animation

“It is art in motion as well as the art of motion” -Jules Engel
  • Pioneered by painters and visual artists from the world of fine art, not movies
  • Focused on the concept of personal expression using animation as a medium
  • An attempt at illustrating the visual language of music and sound
  • Uses light, motion, color and rhythm to evoke emotion

Fine art had been dramatically evolving in Europe and America with the advent of Impressionism (Monet, Van Gogh), Cubism (Picasso) and Expressionism (Kandisnksy).

Early 1920s

A group of German painters became interested in using the medium of film to create animation in order to develop a language of abstract imagery with movement .

These early pioneers (who were all friends and contemporaries of each other) are

  • Walter Ruttman (hand painted film strip to create color effects)
  • Viking Eggeling (interested in visual language)
  • Hans Richter (very influenced by Cubism)

Oskar Fishinger

Oskar Fishinger is one of the most readily recognized and accomplished abstract animators of the 20th century

Walter Ruttman's works
Oskar Fishinger's works

Fischinger shared his wax slicing machine with Ruttman, who licensed it to make abstract shapes for Lotte Reiniger’s “The Adventures of Prince Achmed”

In the mid to late 1920s, Fischinger continued to experiment with multiple projection techniques and film experimentation, and moved to Berlin in 1927. There, he continued to create abstract films, finding distribution in theaters internationally throughout Europe and in Japan and the US. He also did a number of commercials and advertisements.

Fischinger’s most memorable and featured work is his 1935 masterpiece

When Fischinger got an offer from Paramount Studios to move to Hollywood, CA, he and his family escaped the escalating war in Germany (1936).

At Paramount, Fischinger worked on an abstract sequence for the feature film, "The Big Broadcast," which he would later release independently as “Allegretto” (1937)

After Paramount, Fischinger went over to Disney (1939), where he collaborated on the Toccata and Fugue sequence for Fantasia.

Motion Picture Paintings

The last phase of Fischinger's career in the 1940s 50s focused what he called "Motion Picture Paintings" working on panes of glass and filming his painting process brushstroke by brushstroke.

As his health failed, he continued to paint and take the occasional ad work, including a series for Muntz television in the 1950s. However, he slowly moved away from his personal experimental work, and died in 1967 at the age of 66.

Frustrated in his filmmaking, Fischinger turned increasingly to oil painting as a creative outlet. Although the Guggenheim Foundation specifically requested a cel animation film, Fischinger made his Bach film Mostion Painting No.1 (1947) as a documentation of the act of painting, taking a single frame each time he made a brush stroke—and the multi-layered style merely parallels the structure of the Bach music without any tight synchronization. Len Lye

Len Lye was a New Zealand artist who was highly interested in motion as a language of art. He is best known for his direct filmmaking, which would directly influence Norman McLaren’s later work.
