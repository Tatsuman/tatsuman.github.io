Contemporary Television

The 90s Renaissance and Beyond

Overview

  • Beyond feature films, the 1990s would see a renaissance of innovative, creator-driven animated television.
  • The popularity of the 2D feature boom would ripple into the mainstream acceptance of animation in television as marketable for all ages
  • The development of cable allowed networks to appeal to niche audiences, and more “mature” oriented animated programs.

Overview

  • The trend of original, creator-driven animation for a diversity of age groups continued up until the end of the millennium, at which point a multitude of factors would drive studios to reduce budgets, take less risks, and rely more on foreign produced, cheaper animated content.
  • Luckily, this trend has been reversed in recent years with the success of programs like Adventure Time, My Little Pony: Friendship is Magic, and Steven Universe.

The 1980s

The 80s were primarily occupied by merchandise driven television aimed at selling Toys.

Disney launched their first televisionoriented animation series in 1984 with “Wuzzles” and “Adventures of the Gummi Bears,” and would continue on to develop shows featuring classic Disney characters such as “Duck Tales” (1987), “Chip and Dale’s Rescue Rangers,” (1989) and “Tail Spin.” (1990)

Mighty Mouse:The New Adventures

(1987 - 89)

“Mighty Mouse: The New Adventures” (1987) produced by Ralph Bakshi and directed by John Kricfalusi would be the first brief attempt at something stylistically and conceptually different in televised animation, while harkening back to classic, “cartoony” cartoons.

While shortlived, Mighty Mouse not only served as a melting pot of talent of animators and directors who would go on to find success in the later decades, but also as a departure from standard Hanna Barbera and toy-merchandising formulas.

The Simpsons

(1989 - Current)

Meanwhile, the young and unknown FOX network took a chance on the first primetime animated sitcom in decades by greenlighting Matt Groening’s “The Simpsons ” (1989).

Nicktoons and The Ren & Stimpy

(1991 - 1995)

The premiere of Nicktoons in 1991 would officially mark the beginning of creatordriven television animation with KlaskyCsupo’s “Rugrats,” Jim Jinkin’s “Doug” and most significantly, John K’s “Ren and Stimpy.”

“Ren and Stimpy” became a sensation, and showed that subversive, bizarre and twisted animation could be successful. It appealed not only to kids for its grossout, comedic tones, but to adults for the off the wall, surreal writing and style.

One unique trend started by Ren and Stimpy was the “The Gross-Up Close-Up” which consisted of highly rendered, super disgusting closeups.

The success of Nicktoons and the impact of “Ren and Stimpy” motivated other networks and cable channels to begin investing in their own original programming.

Warner Brothers and Batman: TAS

(1992 - 1995)

Steven Spielberg, partnering with Warner Brothers, launched a series of shows harkening back to WB’s classic golden age characters with “Tiny Toon Adventures” (1990) and “Animaniacs.” (1993).

However, the big breakout success for Warner Brothers would be the Emmy winning “Batman: The Animated Series” (1992). The darker tones and sophisticated content proved animation could carry a more serious, action driven look and style. Largely developed by Bruce Timm, the style referenced classic Fleischer Superman cartoons yet gave a unique, creatordriven stamp on one of DC’s more iconic superheros.

While Ren and Stimpy influenced the zany, “cartoony” style of animation, Batman: TAS influenced an entirely new generation of sophisticated action and superhero themed animation.

Cartoon Network was launched as a vehicle for Ted Turner’s massive library of HannaBarbera’s cartoons that he purchased in 1991. While originally airing older theatrical shorts and tv shows, the success of Nicktoons and Warner Brothers spurred CN to create some of their own original programming. Cartoon Network was also the first 24hour single genre channel devoted to showing animated content.

Space Ghost Coast to Coast” was CN’s first original animated programming, and consisted of a mashup between live action and recycled animation from the original 1960s Hanna Barbera cartoon.

In 1995, Fred Seibert’s “Cartoon Cartoons!” launched as a Cartoon Network vehicle to showcase and test out new original series and give animators a chance to pitch and produce shorts. Popular series to come out of this division were

  • “Dexter’s Laboratory” (1995)
  • “Johnny Bravo” (1997)
  • “Cow and Chicken” (1997)
  • “Powerpuff Girls” (1998)
  • “Ed Edd and Eddy” (1999)

Seibert continued on to Nickelodeon with a similar shorts program “Oh Yeah! Cartoons,” which helped launch “Adventure Time,” and “Bravest Warriors,” and now independently as Frederator Studios, most recently behind the series “Bee and Puppycat.”

I Want My MTV

Meanwhile, in New York City, MTV (alongside Children’s Television Workshop, the company behind Sesame Street) would foster and promote a variety of smaller,independent animators to create short “bumpers” and station ID mini-shorts on their station.

A variety of independent animators, such as Henry Sellick (who would continue on to direct StopMotion features) and Bill Plympton (who operates as a successful independent animator) worked on these MTV stings, bringing their small indie animations to a national audience.

MTV’s first official foray into animated programming was “Liquid Television” in 1991. A mashup of live action and animated shorts, it would also serve as a showcase for smaller, independent animators.

Liquid Television spun off two of its more popular concepts into their own series, the anime influenced “Æon Flux” (1991) and the controversial, but popular “Beavis and Butthead.”(1993)

Beavis and Butthead

(1993 - 1996)

Beavis and Butthead were created by an unknown Mike Judge, who would continue on to create the successful long running animated series “King of the Hill” (1997) as well as live-action features (Office Space 1999, Idiocracy 2006).

Beavis and Butthead drew passionate reviews, both positive and negative, from politicians and TV celebrities. Among the positive, it was deemed “a cleverly subversive vehicle for social criticism,” but conservative groups bombasted the series for its immoral, delinquent and sophomoric humor.

Either way, it stands as a symbol of 90s youth culture and as an example of adult-oriented animation at its best.

South Park

(1997 - current)

The success of Beavis and Butthead would directly lead to the creation of another highly popular (and controversial) show, “South Park” (1997), created by Matt Stone and Trey Parker. Another highly subversive and satirical animated series, it launched another young, relatively unknown network, Comedy Central, into the mainstream’s attention. It has been heralded by many to be one of the top tv comedies of all time.

Turn of the Century

The end of the Millennium would see a slew of primetime animated series greenlit, after the success of South Park and King of the Hill (not to mention The Simpsons), such as Family Guy, Futurama, Dilbert and Mission Hill. These would all be cancelled, some to be brought back to find success on other networks (such as Family Guy).

(2001 - present)

The 2000s saw the rise in popularity of adult swim, Cartoon Networks’ late night programming oriented towards the teen and older market.

CN’s original 1994 series, “Space Ghost: Coast to Coast” was originally planned for an older market, but it wouldn’t be until 2000 that additional teen/adult programming would be added with Sealab 2021, Harvey Birdman, Attorney at Law, Aqua Teen Hunger Force and The Brak Show.

adult swim officially launched in 2001 with another network failed primetime series, Brendon Small’s “Home Movies,” and would continue to show both original programming (Robot Chicken, Venture Brothers, Metalocalypse), “orphaned” animated series from other networks (Futurama, Family Guy, Mission Hill, etc), and anime (Cowboy Bebop, Bleach, Fullmetal Alchemist).

Over on Cartoon Network, an anime-inspired series titled Toonami (1997) introduced anime to a slightly younger audience and would trigger the continued wave of anime’s popularity to mainstream American audiences.

Shows that found popularity via Toonami include Dragon Ball Z, Gundam Wing, Sailor Moon, and Naruto.

2000 Recession and Recovery

During the 2000s, a variety of factors emerged that would end the 2D revival and Renaissance boom of the 1990s. In addition to the popularity of cheap anime being imported, the recession and popularity of more cheaply produced liveaction shows (Hannah Montana, etc) in centivised studios to cut back on original animated programming.

Another nail in the coffin for animation was the death of the Saturday Morning Cartoon.

With the rise in cable, the ease of ondemand animation, and a 1997 FCC mandate that required network television to increase “educational” content, the main noncable networks (ABC, NBC, CBS, Fox) began slowly withdrawing their Saturday Morning cartoon lineups.

The last station to cancel its Saturday morning lineup was the CW in September 2014, marking the official “death” of the Saturday Morning Cartoon.

However, even with all these factors in the 2000s that limited animation’s budgets, appeal and creativity, there has been a general reversal in the recent popularity of creator-driven animation, starting with Pendleton Ward’s “Adventure Time,” Lauren Faust’s reconceptualizing of “My Little Pony,” and continuing today with series such as “Steven Universe,” “Rick and Morty” and “Star vs The Forces of Evil.”
